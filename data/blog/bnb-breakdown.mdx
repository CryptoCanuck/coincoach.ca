BNB is the native token of BNB Chain, a smart-contract blockchain closely tied to Binance, the world's largest crypto exchange by trading volume. It began life in 2017 as a simple fee-discount token and has since grown into the fuel for an entire blockchain ecosystem. That close relationship with Binance is BNB's greatest strength — and the source of its most serious criticisms.

## From exchange coupon to its own blockchain

BNB launched in July 2017 through an initial coin offering that raised roughly $15 million. At the time it was called Binance Coin and existed as an **ERC-20 token** — a token issued on the Ethereum blockchain rather than on a chain of its own. Its main job was modest: holders paid lower trading fees on the Binance exchange.

In April 2019, Binance launched its own blockchain and BNB migrated off Ethereum. The bigger step came in September 2020 with BNB Smart Chain, a network that is **EVM-compatible** — meaning it can run the same smart contracts and tools as Ethereum, so developers can move applications over with little rework. In December 2024, the original chain was retired and its functions were folded into BNB Smart Chain, leaving one unified network.

## How BNB Chain reaches consensus

BNB Smart Chain uses **proof of staked authority** — a hybrid system in which a small, fixed-size group of validators takes turns producing blocks, with membership decided by how much BNB each validator and its supporters have staked. According to the official documentation, the network runs 45 active validators: 21 "Cabinet" validators that produce most blocks and 24 "Candidates" that serve as backups, with the set re-elected daily based on staked BNB. Becoming a validator requires a self-stake of at least 2,000 BNB.

This design makes the chain fast and cheap — block times are under a second — but it is a deliberate trade-off. Ethereum has roughly 900,000 validators; BNB Chain has a few dozen. Fewer validators means quicker coordination but far more concentrated control.

## The auto-burn: shrinking supply toward 100 million

BNB started with a supply of 200 million tokens, and Binance committed to destroying half of them over time. A **token burn** is the permanent removal of tokens from circulation by sending them to an unusable address. BNB's quarterly **auto-burn** calculates how many tokens to destroy using a formula based on BNB's price and the number of blocks produced that quarter; a slice of network gas fees is also burned in real time. The 35th quarterly burn, completed in April 2026, removed about 1.57 million BNB and brought total supply down to roughly 134.8 million — still some way from the 100 million target.

## What BNB is used for

BNB's utility centers on the Binance ecosystem and the chain itself:

- Discounted trading fees on the Binance exchange
- Gas fees for transactions and smart contracts on BNB Chain
- Participation in Binance Launchpad token sales
- Collateral and liquidity across BNB Chain's DeFi applications

## The Binance relationship: strength and risk

Binance's enormous user base gives BNB built-in demand that few tokens enjoy. But the dependence cuts both ways. In November 2023, Binance pleaded guilty to U.S. charges including anti-money-laundering failures and sanctions violations, agreeing to pay about $4.3 billion. Founder Changpeng Zhao pleaded guilty personally, paid a $50 million fine, stepped down as CEO, and served a four-month sentence. He received a presidential pardon in October 2025 — itself a politically contested decision. The episode showed that legal trouble at one company can shake the entire BNB ecosystem.

Centralization criticism extends to the chain. With only 45 validators, coordinated action is easy: in October 2022, validators paused the whole network after an exploit of its cross-chain bridge. That responsiveness has an upside, but it also means the chain lacks the censorship resistance many people expect from a blockchain.

## Risks

BNB carries the usual crypto volatility, plus concentrated exposure to a single company's regulatory and reputational fortunes. Smart-contract bugs on BNB Chain applications remain a real hazard, and the small validator set means users must trust a narrow group of operators.

## In summary

BNB evolved from a fee-discount coupon into the backbone of a major smart-contract network with a steadily shrinking supply. Its speed, low costs, and Binance integration are genuine advantages; its centralization and dependence on one company are genuine weaknesses. Anyone evaluating BNB is, in large part, evaluating Binance itself. This article is for educational purposes only and is not financial advice.

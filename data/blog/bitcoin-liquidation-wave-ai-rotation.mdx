Bitcoin's slide below $60,000 earlier this month was not just a price decline — it was a forced unwind. The roughly 14 percent drop over the first week of June triggered close to $10 billion in liquidations of long futures positions, one of the largest such flushes of the year. By Tuesday, June 9, the price had stabilized near $63,000, leaving analysts to debate what actually drove the move.

## What a liquidation wave is

Futures traders can use **leverage** — borrowed money that multiplies both gains and losses. When the market moves against a leveraged position far enough, the exchange forcibly closes it, a process called **liquidation**. Forced selling pushes the price down further, which liquidates more positions, and the cascade feeds itself. That mechanical chain reaction is why crypto declines so often arrive fast and steep: nearly $10 billion in bullish bets were closed out involuntarily in roughly a week.

Positioning had been rebuilt aggressively before the break. Open interest in Bitcoin futures — the total value of outstanding contracts — had climbed back toward levels last seen before February's lows, leaving plenty of leverage to unwind once prices slipped.

## The AI rotation underneath

The trigger matters less than the tide. An estimated $400 billion has flowed into artificial-intelligence infrastructure over the past six months, and AI-linked stocks and private deals have become the dominant high-growth trade. For many funds, Bitcoin and AI occupy the same risk bucket — and right now AI is winning the competition for capital.

The clearest evidence is in the spot Bitcoin ETFs. The funds have bled roughly $4 billion since mid-May, and last week extended a record streak of consecutive daily net outflows, with total assets across the funds falling from about $108 billion in mid-May to roughly $83 billion. Regulatory filings show hedge funds cutting their share of the largest fund, BlackRock's IBIT, from about 29 percent to 19 percent over recent months. Strategy executive chairman Michael Saylor — whose company holds the largest corporate Bitcoin treasury — has himself described the selloff as an AI rotation rather than a verdict on Bitcoin.

## What it means for a general reader

Two takeaways are worth separating. The first is mechanical: when leverage is crowded, ordinary declines become violent ones, and headlines about billions "wiped out" mostly describe traders' borrowed bets, not money leaving the ecosystem permanently. The second is structural: Bitcoin's price now competes for institutional capital against other technology trades, which means it can fall for reasons that have nothing to do with crypto itself.

Whether the flush marks a bottom is unknowable in advance — forced sellers are gone, but the capital rotation that set the stage has not reversed. As always, this article describes what happened and makes no prediction about what comes next.
